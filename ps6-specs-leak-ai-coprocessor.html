PS6 Specs Leak: Sony Next-Gen Console Leverages Custom AI Co-Processor for Native 8K 120FPS

By Harbr Editorial Desk • July 14, 2026 • 5 min read

The hardware enthusiast community has been set ablaze following a massive supply-chain leak detailing the architectural blueprint of Sony's highly anticipated PlayStation 6 (PS6). The leak, originating from key manufacturing hubs in Taiwan, provides the first comprehensive look at the silicon that will power the next generation of console gaming, pointing to a release window in late 2028.

The Silicon Core: AMD Zen 6 & RDNA 5

According to leaked documentation, the PS6 will continue Sony's partnership with AMD, utilizing a highly customized system-on-a-chip (SoC) fabricated on TSMC's ultra-advanced 2nm process node. At the heart of this chip lies a 12-core AMD Zen 6 CPU, paired with an RDNA 5-based graphics subsystem boasting 84 Compute Units (CUs).

This layout yields a raw performance projection of over 38 Teraflops of graphics computing power—more than double that of the PS5 Pro. However, the raw computing specs only tell half the story. The true differentiator of the PlayStation 6 is its integrated co-processing hardware.

Enter PSSR 2.0: Dedicated AI Co-Processor

Following the introduction of PlayStation Spectral Super Resolution (PSSR) in the PS5 Pro, Sony is doubling down on artificial intelligence. The leak reveals a dedicated NPU (Neural Processing Unit) co-processor co-developed with AMD, boasting 120 TOPS (Trillions of Operations Per Second) of machine learning performance.

This dedicated processor will handle PSSR 2.0, a hardware-level machine learning upscaling algorithm that reconstructs sub-4K render targets into native-quality 8K outputs. By offloading upscaling entirely to the NPU, the main RDNA 5 GPU is freed up to run ray-tracing calculations and high-fidelity physics simulations at 120 frames per second.

"By offloading upscaling entirely to the dedicated NPU, developers can allocate the main GPU to run complex ray-tracing pipelines at high refresh rates without sacrificing resolution."
— Hardware Analyst Desk

Ultra-Fast Memory Architecture

To eliminate memory bandwidth bottlenecks, Sony is reportedly utilizing 24GB of unified GDDR7 memory, operating across a 384-bit bus. This memory subsystem delivers a bandwidth of over 1.2 TB/s, enabling near-instantaneous asset streaming from the console's custom PCIe Gen 6 NVMe SSD. Loading screens are expected to be completely eliminated, transitioning to instantaneous game boots.

Target Price and Release Expectations

Industry analysts project that the advanced 2nm silicon node and GDDR7 memory will push manufacturing costs higher. The PlayStation 6 is expected to target an introductory retail price of $599 to $649. While Sony has not officially commented on the leaks, supply-chain orders suggest that pre-production testing of the AMD silicon will commence in early 2027, aligning with a holiday 2028 launch timeline.
